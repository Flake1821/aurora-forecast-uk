Night Sky Events 2026

Major astronomical events visible from the UK, in chronological order

{# ── JANUARY ── #}
{{ svg_event('meteor', 1) }}
Meteor Shower
Quadrantids Meteor Shower Peak
3–4 Jan

The Quadrantids are one of the strongest annual meteor showers, capable of producing up to 120 meteors per hour under ideal conditions. The radiant lies in the constellation Boötes, near the defunct constellation Quadrans Muralis.

Best viewing: After midnight on 3 January into pre-dawn on 4 January. Look towards the north-east sky.

UK visibility: Good from all parts of the UK. The peak is brief (around 6 hours) so timing is important.

Tips: The waxing crescent Moon sets early, leaving dark skies for the peak. Find a dark site away from light pollution and allow 20 minutes for your eyes to adapt.

{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 2) }}
Full Moon
Full Wolf Moon
13 Jan

January's full Moon is traditionally known as the Wolf Moon, named after the howling wolves heard during cold midwinter nights. It rises around sunset and sets around sunrise, illuminating the long winter night.

Best viewing: Visible all night from dusk to dawn.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. January's full Moon rides high across the sky due to the Moon's orbit tilting opposite to the Sun's low winter path.

Tips: A full Moon is a poor night for meteor or deep-sky observing, but ideal for moonlit winter landscape photography.

{# ── FEBRUARY ── #}
{{ svg_event('planet', 3) }}
Planet
Venus at Greatest Eastern Elongation
1 Feb

Venus reaches its greatest angular separation from the Sun (47.2°), making it a brilliant evening star visible in the western sky after sunset.

Best viewing: Look west-southwest after sunset. Venus will be clearly visible for several hours before setting.

UK visibility: Excellent from all UK locations. At magnitude –4.4, Venus is unmistakable — by far the brightest point of light in the sky.

Tips: Through a small telescope, Venus will appear as a half-lit disc similar to a first-quarter Moon. Try photographing Venus alongside twilight landscapes for dramatic results.

{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 4) }}
Full Moon
Full Snow Moon
12 Feb

February's full Moon is known as the Snow Moon, reflecting the typically heavy snowfalls of midwinter in the Northern Hemisphere.

Best viewing: Visible all night, rising in the east around sunset.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. Like January's Wolf Moon, it tracks high across the sky during the long winter night.

Tips: Ideal for photographing moonlit snowy landscapes. Use a telephoto lens at moonrise for dramatic scale against foreground features.

{# ── MARCH ── #}
{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 5) }}
Full Moon
Full Worm Moon
14 Mar

The Worm Moon marks the transition to spring, named for the earthworm casts that appear as the ground begins to thaw.

Best viewing: Visible all night from dusk to dawn.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations.

Tips: As we approach the equinox, the Moon's path across the sky becomes more moderate in altitude compared to winter full Moons.

{{ svg_event('equinox', 6) }}
Season
March Equinox
20 Mar

The vernal equinox marks the astronomical start of spring in the Northern Hemisphere. Day and night are approximately equal in length.

Best viewing: Not an observational event, but sunset and sunrise are due east and west — useful for calibrating your bearings at your observing site.

UK visibility: From this date, nights become shorter and the window for astronomical observation begins to shrink.

Tips: Equinox evenings are excellent for spotting the zodiacal light — a faint pyramid of light along the ecliptic visible in the west after twilight from dark sites.

{{ svg_event('eclipse', 7) }}
Eclipse
Total Lunar Eclipse
29 Mar

A total lunar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes entirely through Earth's umbral shadow, turning a deep copper-red colour — a so-called “Blood Moon.”

Best viewing: The eclipse begins in the early hours. From the UK, the Moon will be setting during totality, so look low to the west-southwest.

UK visibility: Partially visible from the UK — the early penumbral and partial phases are visible, but the Moon sets around totality. Western UK and higher elevations have the best chance of glimpsing the start of totality near the horizon.

Tips: A low eclipse near the horizon can produce stunning photographs with foreground elements. Use a telephoto lens and find a location with a clear western horizon.

{# ── APRIL ── #}
{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 8) }}
Full Moon
Full Pink Moon
12 Apr

The Pink Moon is named after the pink phlox wildflowers that bloom in early spring across North America — the Moon itself does not appear pink.

Best viewing: Visible all night, rising around sunset in the east.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations.

Tips: April's full Moon sits lower in the sky than winter full Moons. Try capturing it at moonrise when it appears large near the horizon due to the “Moon illusion.”

{{ svg_event('meteor', 9) }}
Meteor Shower
Lyrids Meteor Shower Peak
21–22 Apr

The Lyrids are one of the oldest recorded meteor showers, observed for over 2,700 years. They produce around 18 meteors per hour at peak, with occasional surges of up to 100.

Best viewing: After midnight on 21 April into pre-dawn on 22 April. The radiant is near the bright star Vega in the constellation Lyra, which rises in the north-east.

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations. Vega is well-placed in the pre-dawn sky.

Tips: The waning crescent Moon will not interfere. Lyrid meteors are often bright with persistent trains — some fireballs can cast brief shadows.

{# ── MAY ── #}
{{ svg_event('meteor', 10) }}
Meteor Shower
Eta Aquariids Meteor Shower Peak
5–6 May

The Eta Aquariids originate from debris left by Halley's Comet. While they produce up to 50 meteors per hour in the Southern Hemisphere, UK observers typically see 10–20 per hour as the radiant is low.

Best viewing: Pre-dawn hours on 5–6 May, looking south-east. The radiant (in Aquarius) doesn't rise high above the UK horizon.

UK visibility: Moderate — best from southern England where the radiant reaches a higher altitude. Meteors from this shower are very fast (66 km/s) and often leave persistent trains.

Tips: Despite the low radiant, earthgrazers — long, bright meteors streaking horizontally — can appear across the sky. Best observed from the darkest sites you can reach.

{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 11) }}
Full Moon
Full Flower Moon
12 May

May's full Moon is the Flower Moon, named for the abundance of wildflowers blooming across the countryside.

Best viewing: Visible from dusk, tracking low across the southern sky.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations, though it stays lower in the sky as we approach summer.

Tips: Late spring's warm evenings make this an enjoyable full Moon to observe. The low angle through more atmosphere can give it a golden or amber tint near the horizon.

{# ── JUNE ── #}
{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 12) }}
Full Moon
Full Strawberry Moon
10 Jun

The Strawberry Moon coincides with the strawberry harvest season. June's full Moon often appears amber or reddish as it tracks very low across the sky.

Best viewing: Visible from late evening, staying very close to the southern horizon.

UK visibility: Visible from all locations but at its lowest altitude of the year. The Moon barely rises above the rooftops from northern Scotland.

Tips: The extremely low path makes this Moon naturally golden-amber all night. Perfect for creative photography with foreground landmarks silhouetted against a large, warm-toned Moon.

{{ svg_event('equinox', 13) }}
Season
June Solstice — Longest Day
20 Jun

The summer solstice marks the longest day and shortest night of the year. The Sun reaches its highest point in the sky and the most northerly sunset/sunrise positions.

Best viewing: Sunrise and sunset celebrations — many people gather at ancient sites like Stonehenge. In northern Scotland, twilight persists through the “night.”

UK visibility: London enjoys approximately 16 hours 38 minutes of daylight. In the Shetland Islands, there is no true darkness — the “simmer dim” persists all night.

Tips: The worst time of year for deep-sky astronomy, but noctilucent clouds — electric-blue ice clouds at 80 km altitude — begin their season and are best seen from the UK around solstice. Look north, 1–2 hours after sunset.

{# ── JULY ── #}
{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 14) }}
Full Moon
Full Buck Moon
10 Jul

The Buck Moon is named for the time when male deer begin growing their new antlers. Like June, this full Moon stays low in the sky.

Best viewing: Visible from late evening through dawn, tracking close to the southern horizon.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations, though it remains at a low altitude throughout the short summer night.

Tips: Summer full Moons are ideal for wide-angle landscape photography. The short nights mean the Moon can be captured in twilight conditions with attractive colours.

{{ svg_event('meteor', 15) }}
Meteor Shower
Delta Aquariids Meteor Shower Peak
28–29 Jul

The Southern Delta Aquariids produce around 15–20 meteors per hour at peak. Although better from southern latitudes, UK observers can catch meteors low in the southern sky.

Best viewing: After midnight, looking towards the south. The radiant is in the constellation Aquarius.

UK visibility: Moderate — rates are lower from the UK due to the southern radiant. Southern England has an advantage.

Tips: This shower overlaps with the early Perseids, so you may see meteors from both radiants in the same session. The waxing crescent Moon sets early, leaving dark skies.

{# ── AUGUST ── #}
{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 16) }}
Full Moon
Full Sturgeon Moon
8 Aug

August's full Moon is the Sturgeon Moon, named by Native American fishing tribes for the large sturgeon fish caught at this time of year in the Great Lakes.

Best viewing: Visible from late evening, rising in the south-east.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. As summer progresses, full Moons begin to climb slightly higher again.

Tips: Unfortunately for 2026, this Moon falls just days before the Perseid peak, so its brightness will significantly reduce visible meteor rates. Plan Perseid observing before and after full Moon.

{{ svg_event('meteor', 17) }}
Meteor Shower
Perseids Meteor Shower Peak
12 Aug

The Perseids are the most popular annual meteor shower, producing up to 100 meteors per hour under ideal conditions. They originate from Comet 109P/Swift–Tuttle and are renowned for bright fireballs.

Best viewing: After midnight on 12 August into pre-dawn on 13 August. The radiant is in Perseus, rising high in the north-east.

UK visibility: Excellent from all UK locations. August's warm evenings and the high radiant altitude make this the most comfortable meteor shower to observe.

Tips: In 2026, the nearly-full Moon will be a significant issue, washing out fainter meteors. Focus on the brightest fireballs and try shielding the Moon behind a building or tree. The shower is active 17 Jul – 24 Aug, so try nights away from full Moon too.

{{ svg_event('planet', 18) }}
Planet
Saturn at Opposition
14 Aug

Saturn reaches opposition — directly opposite the Sun — making it visible all night, rising at sunset and setting at sunrise. It shines at its brightest for the year.

Best viewing: Visible all night, highest in the south around midnight. Saturn will be in the constellation Pisces/Aquarius.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. Opposition occurs when Earth is closest to Saturn, making its rings and moons most visible through a telescope.

Tips: Even a small telescope (60mm+) will reveal Saturn's iconic rings. 2026 sees the rings nearly edge-on as Saturn approaches its ring-plane crossing — a rare geometric event. A good pair of binoculars will show Saturn as a slightly elongated disc.

{# ── SEPTEMBER ── #}
{{ svg_event('eclipse', 19) }}
Eclipse
Total Lunar Eclipse — Harvest Moon
7 Sep

A total lunar eclipse coincides with the Harvest Moon — a spectacular combination. The Moon will turn deep red as it passes through Earth's shadow, the iconic “Blood Moon” effect.

Best viewing: The entire eclipse is visible from the UK. Totality occurs during evening hours — prime time for watching. The Moon rises already in partial eclipse.

UK visibility: Fully visible from the entire UK — the best lunar eclipse of 2026 for British observers. Totality lasts approximately 1 hour 22 minutes.

Tips: This is the headline event of 2026 for UK stargazers. Find a location with a clear south-eastern horizon for moonrise. Binoculars enhance the colour and detail. During totality, the background stars become visible, making for superb wide-angle photographs.

{{ svg_event('equinox', 20) }}
Season
September Equinox
22 Sep

The autumnal equinox marks the start of astronomical autumn. Day and night are approximately equal, and from this date the nights grow longer than the days.

Best viewing: Sunrise and sunset occur due east and west respectively.

UK visibility: The equinox signals the return of longer nights — excellent news for astronomers and aurora hunters. Dark skies return to northern Scotland.

Tips: The equinox period is also prime time for aurora activity. Geomagnetic storms are statistically more common around the equinoxes due to the orientation of Earth's magnetic field relative to the solar wind.

{{ svg_event('planet', 21) }}
Planet
Neptune at Opposition
23 Sep

Neptune reaches opposition, shining at its brightest for the year at magnitude +7.8. It remains invisible to the naked eye but is reachable through binoculars and telescopes.

Best viewing: Visible most of the night in the constellation Pisces. Highest around midnight.

UK visibility: Good from all locations with optical aid. You'll need at least 7×50 binoculars and a detailed star chart to identify it among the background stars.

Tips: Through a telescope at 100×+ magnification, Neptune appears as a tiny blue-green disc — distinct from the pinpoint stars around it. Its largest moon Triton can be spotted in larger amateur telescopes.

{# ── OCTOBER ── #}
{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 22) }}
Full Moon
Full Hunter's Moon
7 Oct

The Hunter's Moon is the first full Moon after the Harvest Moon. Historically, it marked the time to hunt and stockpile meat before winter.

Best viewing: Visible all night, rising around sunset. October full Moons often appear large and orange at the horizon due to the shallow angle of the ecliptic.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. The Moon climbs higher each month now as we approach winter.

Tips: The Hunter's Moon rises at nearly the same time on successive evenings (unlike most months), so you get several evenings of “full-looking” moonrise in a row.

{{ svg_event('meteor', 23) }}
Meteor Shower
Orionids Meteor Shower Peak
21–22 Oct

The Orionids are the second meteor shower produced by Halley's Comet debris (the Eta Aquariids being the first). They produce around 20 meteors per hour at peak.

Best viewing: After midnight on 21 October into pre-dawn on 22 October. The radiant is near Betelgeuse in Orion, which rises in the east.

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations. Orion is well-placed in the autumn pre-dawn sky.

Tips: Orionid meteors are fast (66 km/s) and often produce persistent trains that glow for several seconds. The waning crescent Moon will not be a problem for this shower.

{# ── NOVEMBER ── #}
{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 24) }}
Full Moon
Full Beaver Moon
5 Nov

November's full Moon is the Beaver Moon, named for the time when beaver traps were traditionally set before waterways froze over.

Best viewing: Visible all night, rising in the east around sunset. November's Moon climbs high in the sky during the long autumn night.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations.

Tips: Watch for Bonfire Night fireworks lighting up the sky beneath the full Moon — a uniquely British spectacle! The bright moonlight will compete with all but the largest fireworks displays.

{{ svg_event('meteor', 25) }}
Meteor Shower
Leonids Meteor Shower Peak
17–18 Nov

The Leonids are famous for producing periodic meteor storms (most recently in 1999 and 2001). In a normal year, expect around 15 meteors per hour. The parent comet is 55P/Tempel–Tuttle.

Best viewing: After midnight on 17 November into pre-dawn on 18 November. The radiant is in Leo, rising in the east.

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations. Leo is well above the horizon by 1am.

Tips: Leonid meteors are among the fastest at 71 km/s, often producing bright fireballs with vivid colours. The waning crescent Moon rises late and won't interfere significantly.

{{ svg_event('planet', 26) }}
Planet
Mercury at Greatest Eastern Elongation
24 Nov

Mercury reaches its greatest angular separation from the Sun in the evening sky (22.0°). As the innermost planet, Mercury is notoriously difficult to observe.

Best viewing: Look low in the south-west immediately after sunset, about 30–45 minutes after the Sun goes down. Mercury will appear as a moderately bright yellowish “star.”

UK visibility: Moderate — autumn evening elongations are reasonably good from UK latitudes as the ecliptic makes a steep angle with the horizon.

Tips: Use binoculars to find Mercury in the twilight glow, then try to spot it with the naked eye. Many experienced astronomers have never seen Mercury — this is your chance!

{# ── DECEMBER ── #}
{{ svg_event('fullmoon', 27) }}
Full Moon
Full Cold Moon
4 Dec

The Cold Moon marks the onset of winter. December's full Moon rides high across the sky during the longest nights of the year — the mirror of June's low Strawberry Moon.

Best viewing: Visible all night, reaching a very high altitude as it crosses the sky. From northern UK locations, it passes almost directly overhead.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations.

Tips: The Cold Moon is one of the highest full Moons of the year, casting sharp shadows and brilliantly illuminating winter landscapes. Unfortunately it falls just before the Geminid peak.

{{ svg_event('meteor', 28) }}
Meteor Shower
Geminids Meteor Shower Peak
13–14 Dec

The Geminids are widely considered the best meteor shower of the year, producing up to 150 multicoloured meteors per hour under ideal conditions. Unusually, they originate from an asteroid — 3200 Phaethon — rather than a comet.

Best viewing: From around 9pm on 13 December through pre-dawn on 14 December. The radiant in Gemini is well-placed by mid-evening, rising in the east-northeast.

UK visibility: Excellent from all UK locations. The Geminid radiant rises early, giving a long observing window. Rates increase steadily through the night.

Tips: In 2026, the waning gibbous Moon (about 75% illuminated) will wash out fainter meteors during the second half of the night. Focus your observing before midnight when the Moon hasn't yet risen. Geminid meteors are slower than Perseids and come in a range of colours — white, yellow, green, and blue.

{{ svg_event('equinox', 29) }}
Season
December Solstice — Shortest Day
21 Dec

The winter solstice is the shortest day and longest night of the year — the Sun reaches its lowest noontime altitude. From London, there are only about 7 hours 50 minutes of daylight.

Best viewing: The longest night of the year — perfect for extended observing sessions. Astronomical darkness begins early and lasts until morning.

UK visibility: The solstice marks the peak of the observing season. Over 16 hours of darkness from southern England, and even more from Scotland.

Tips: Wrap up warm and make the most of the long night. The winter Milky Way is spectacular, Orion dominates the south, and the aurora season continues. Many observing groups hold all-night solstice events.

{{ svg_event('meteor', 30) }}
Meteor Shower
Ursids Meteor Shower Peak
22 Dec

The Ursids are the final meteor shower of the year, producing around 5–10 meteors per hour. They radiate from near the star Kochab in Ursa Minor (the Little Bear).

Best viewing: Pre-dawn hours on 22 December. The radiant is circumpolar from the UK, meaning it's visible all night.

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations — the circumpolar radiant gives UK observers an advantage over southern latitudes.

Tips: Though modest in rates, the Ursids occasionally produce unexpected outbursts. The waxing crescent Moon sets early, leaving excellent dark skies for the longest night of the year. A fitting end to the 2026 observing calendar.
