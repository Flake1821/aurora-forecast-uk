{# ── JANUARY ── #}
Meteor Shower
Quadrantids Meteor Shower Peak
3–4 Jan

The Quadrantids are one of the strongest annual meteor showers, capable of producing up to 120 meteors per hour under ideal conditions. However, in 2026 the full Supermoon on the same night will significantly reduce visible rates to around 40 per hour. The radiant lies in the constellation Boötes, near the defunct constellation Quadrans Muralis.

Best viewing: After midnight on 3 January into pre-dawn on 4 January. Look towards the north-east sky.

UK visibility: Good from all parts of the UK, though the bright Supermoon will wash out fainter meteors. The peak is brief (around 6 hours) so timing is important.

Tips: Try to shield the bright Moon behind a building or tree. Focus on the brighter fireballs. Find a dark site away from light pollution and allow 20 minutes for your eyes to adapt.

Full Moon
Full Wolf Moon (Supermoon)
3 Jan

The first full Moon of 2026 is a Supermoon — appearing slightly larger and brighter than average as the Moon is near its closest point to Earth (perigee). January’s full Moon is traditionally known as the Wolf Moon, named after the howling wolves heard during cold midwinter nights.

Best viewing: Visible all night from dusk to dawn. The Supermoon rises around sunset and sets around sunrise, illuminating the long winter night.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. January’s full Moon rides high across the sky due to the Moon’s orbit tilting opposite to the Sun’s low winter path.

Tips: A Supermoon is a great night for moonlit winter landscape photography, but a poor night for meteor or deep-sky observing. Unfortunately it coincides with the Quadrantid peak.

Planet
Jupiter at Opposition
10 Jan

Jupiter reaches opposition — directly opposite the Sun in the sky — making it visible all night, rising at sunset and setting at sunrise. It shines at a brilliant magnitude −2.7, the brightest it will be all year.

Best viewing: Visible all night in the constellation Gemini, highest in the south around midnight. At a distance of 4.23 AU, its disc spans 45.6 arcseconds.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. Gemini is high in the winter sky, placing Jupiter at a favourable altitude for observation.

Tips: Even modest binoculars (10×50) will reveal Jupiter’s four Galilean moons — Io, Europa, Ganymede and Callisto — as tiny points of light flanking the planet. A small telescope (60mm+) shows the two main cloud bands and the Great Red Spot during favourable transits.

{# ── FEBRUARY ── #}
Full Moon
Full Snow Moon
1 Feb

February’s full Moon is known as the Snow Moon, reflecting the typically heavy snowfalls of midwinter in the Northern Hemisphere.

Best viewing: Visible all night, rising in the east around sunset.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. Like January’s Wolf Moon, it tracks high across the sky during the long winter night.

Tips: Ideal for photographing moonlit snowy landscapes. Use a telephoto lens at moonrise for dramatic scale against foreground features.

Eclipse
Annular Solar Eclipse
17 Feb

An annular solar eclipse — the “ring of fire” — occurs when the Moon passes in front of the Sun but is too far away to cover it completely, leaving a bright ring of sunlight around the Moon’s silhouette.

Best viewing: The annular path crosses Antarctica and the southern Atlantic and Indian Oceans. A partial eclipse is visible from parts of southern Africa, South America and Australia.

UK visibility: Not visible from the UK. The eclipse occurs entirely in the Southern Hemisphere.

Tips: While this one is not for us, mark your calendar for 12 August 2026 — a total solar eclipse whose partial phase will be spectacular from Cornwall and across the UK.

Conjunction
Saturn–Neptune Conjunction
20 Feb

A rare Saturn–Neptune conjunction — the two planets appear within 0°49′ of each other in the sky. This event occurs roughly once every 36 years, making it a genuinely rare alignment.

Best viewing: Look low in the western sky approximately 30–45 minutes after sunset. Saturn (magnitude +1.0) is visible to the naked eye; Neptune (magnitude +7.9) requires binoculars or a telescope.

UK visibility: Challenging but possible. The pair will be very low on the western horizon in the constellation Pisces. A clear, unobstructed western horizon is essential.

Tips: Through a telescope at moderate magnification, both planets can be seen in the same field of view — a genuinely special sight. This is the first of three conjunctions in a rare triple conjunction event spanning 2025–2026.

Alignment
Six-Planet Alignment
28 Feb

A spectacular six-planet alignment stretches across the evening sky: Mercury, Venus, Saturn, Neptune, Uranus and Jupiter are all visible after sunset, strung out along the ecliptic from the western to the south-eastern horizon.

Best viewing: 30–60 minutes after sunset. Jupiter will be high in the south-east in Gemini, while Mercury, Venus, Saturn and Neptune cluster low in the west.

UK visibility: Good for the four naked-eye planets (Mercury, Venus, Saturn, Jupiter). Uranus (magnitude +5.7) and Neptune (magnitude +7.9) require binoculars or a telescope.

Tips: Find a location with a clear, unobstructed western horizon. Start looking for Mercury and Venus first as they set quickly. Use a planetarium app to identify the fainter planets. An excellent opportunity for a wide-angle panoramic photograph.

{# ── MARCH ── #}
Full Moon
Full Worm Moon
3 Mar

The Worm Moon marks the transition to spring, named for the earthworm casts that appear as the ground begins to thaw. This full Moon coincides with a total lunar eclipse, though unfortunately the eclipse is not visible from the UK.

Best viewing: Visible all night from dusk to dawn.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. The full Moon itself is perfectly visible; only the eclipse is below our horizon.

Tips: As we approach the equinox, the Moon’s path across the sky becomes more moderate in altitude compared to winter full Moons.

Eclipse
Total Lunar Eclipse
3 Mar

A total lunar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes entirely through Earth’s umbral shadow, turning a deep copper-red colour — a so-called “Blood Moon.” This eclipse coincides with the full Worm Moon.

Best viewing: The eclipse is visible from eastern Asia, Australia, the Pacific and the Americas. Totality lasts approximately 58 minutes.

UK visibility: Not visible from the UK. The Moon will be below the horizon during the eclipse. By the time the Moon rises over Britain, the eclipse will have ended.

Tips: Don’t worry — the UK gets a partial lunar eclipse on 28 August 2026 that is fully visible. Online live streams will cover this March eclipse if you want to watch remotely.

Conjunction
Venus–Saturn Conjunction
8 Mar

Venus passes approximately 1° from Saturn in the evening sky — a striking pairing of the brilliant “evening star” (magnitude −3.8) alongside the fainter ringed planet (magnitude +1.0).

Best viewing: Look west-southwest approximately 30–45 minutes after sunset. The pair will be low on the horizon in Pisces.

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations. Venus is unmistakable and will guide you straight to Saturn beside it.

Tips: Binoculars will show both planets in the same field of view — a lovely sight. Through a telescope, you can see Venus as a gibbous disc alongside Saturn’s rings (nearly edge-on in 2026).

Season
March Equinox
20 Mar

The vernal equinox marks the astronomical start of spring in the Northern Hemisphere. Day and night are approximately equal in length.

Best viewing: Not an observational event, but sunset and sunrise are due east and west — useful for calibrating your bearings at your observing site.

UK visibility: From this date, nights become shorter and the window for astronomical observation begins to shrink.

Tips: Equinox evenings are excellent for spotting the zodiacal light — a faint pyramid of light along the ecliptic visible in the west after twilight from dark sites.

{# ── APRIL ── #}
Full Moon
Full Pink Moon
2 Apr

The Pink Moon is named after the pink phlox wildflowers that bloom in early spring across North America — the Moon itself does not appear pink.

Best viewing: Visible all night, rising around sunset in the east.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations.

Tips: April’s full Moon sits lower in the sky than winter full Moons. Try capturing it at moonrise when it appears large near the horizon due to the “Moon illusion.”

Meteor Shower
Lyrids Meteor Shower Peak
22–23 Apr

The Lyrids are one of the oldest recorded meteor showers, observed for over 2,700 years. They produce around 18 meteors per hour at peak, with occasional surges of up to 100.

Best viewing: After midnight on 22 April into pre-dawn on 23 April. The radiant is near the bright star Vega in the constellation Lyra, which rises in the north-east.

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations. Vega is well-placed in the pre-dawn sky.

Tips: The waning crescent Moon will not interfere. Lyrid meteors are often bright with persistent trains — some fireballs can cast brief shadows.

{# ── MAY ── #}
Full Moon
Full Flower Moon
1 May

May’s first full Moon is the Flower Moon, named for the abundance of wildflowers blooming across the countryside.

Best viewing: Visible from dusk, tracking low across the southern sky.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations, though it stays lower in the sky as we approach summer.

Tips: Late spring’s warm evenings make this an enjoyable full Moon to observe. The low angle through more atmosphere can give it a golden or amber tint near the horizon. Note: 2026 is a rare 13-moon year — a second full Moon (the Blue Moon) follows on 31 May.

Meteor Shower
Eta Aquariids Meteor Shower Peak
5–6 May

The Eta Aquariids originate from debris left by Halley’s Comet. While they produce up to 50 meteors per hour in the Southern Hemisphere, UK observers typically see 10–20 per hour as the radiant is low.

Best viewing: Pre-dawn hours on 5–6 May, looking south-east. The radiant (in Aquarius) doesn’t rise high above the UK horizon.

UK visibility: Moderate — best from southern England where the radiant reaches a higher altitude. Meteors from this shower are very fast (66 km/s) and often leave persistent trains.

Tips: Despite the low radiant, earthgrazers — long, bright meteors streaking horizontally — can appear across the sky. Best observed from the darkest sites you can reach.

Full Moon
Full Blue Moon
31 May

The second full Moon in a calendar month is known as a Blue Moon — the origin of the phrase “once in a blue moon.” 2026 is a rare 13-moon year, and this is the extra one.

Best viewing: Visible from late evening, tracking low across the southern sky like the Flower Moon before it.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. Despite its name, the Moon does not actually appear blue — it looks the same as any other full Moon.

Tips: Blue Moons occur roughly once every 2.7 years. This one is also a Micromoon (near apogee), so it will appear slightly smaller than average — the opposite of a Supermoon. A fun talking point for moonlit gatherings.

{# ── JUNE ── #}
Conjunction
Venus–Jupiter Conjunction
9 Jun

The two brightest planets in the sky appear close together — a dazzling “double planet” display. Venus shines at magnitude −3.8 alongside Jupiter at magnitude −1.8, creating a spectacular pair visible to the naked eye.

Best viewing: Look low in the western sky approximately 30–60 minutes after sunset. The pair will be in the constellation Gemini.

UK visibility: Good from all locations. No special equipment needed — the two planets are unmistakable as the brightest objects in the twilight sky.

Tips: A wonderful photo opportunity. Use a DSLR or smartphone with a good zoom to capture both planets together with the twilight horizon. Binoculars will show Jupiter’s moons alongside dazzling Venus.

Season
June Solstice — Longest Day
21 Jun

The summer solstice marks the longest day and shortest night of the year. The Sun reaches its highest point in the sky and the most northerly sunset/sunrise positions.

Best viewing: Sunrise and sunset celebrations — many people gather at ancient sites like Stonehenge. In northern Scotland, twilight persists through the “night.”

UK visibility: London enjoys approximately 16 hours 38 minutes of daylight. In the Shetland Islands, there is no true darkness — the “simmer dim” persists all night.

Tips: The worst time of year for deep-sky astronomy, but noctilucent clouds — electric-blue ice clouds at 80 km altitude — begin their season and are best seen from the UK around solstice. Look north, 1–2 hours after sunset.

Full Moon
Full Strawberry Moon (Micromoon)
30 Jun

The Strawberry Moon coincides with the strawberry harvest season. June’s full Moon is also a Micromoon — occurring near apogee (the Moon’s farthest point from Earth), making it appear slightly smaller and less bright than average.

Best viewing: Visible from late evening, staying very close to the southern horizon.

UK visibility: Visible from all locations but at its lowest altitude of the year. The Moon barely rises above the rooftops from northern Scotland.

Tips: The extremely low path makes this Moon naturally golden-amber all night. Perfect for creative photography with foreground landmarks silhouetted against a large, warm-toned Moon.

{# ── JULY ── #}
Full Moon
Full Buck Moon
29 Jul

The Buck Moon is named for the time when male deer begin growing their new antlers. Like June, this full Moon stays low in the sky.

Best viewing: Visible from late evening through dawn, tracking close to the southern horizon.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations, though it remains at a low altitude throughout the short summer night.

Tips: Summer full Moons are ideal for wide-angle landscape photography. The short nights mean the Moon can be captured in twilight conditions with attractive colours.

Meteor Shower
Delta Aquariids Meteor Shower Peak
30–31 Jul

The Southern Delta Aquariids produce around 15–20 meteors per hour at peak. Although better from southern latitudes, UK observers can catch meteors low in the southern sky.

Best viewing: After midnight, looking towards the south. The radiant is in the constellation Aquarius.

UK visibility: Moderate — rates are lower from the UK due to the southern radiant. Southern England has an advantage.

Tips: This shower overlaps with the early Perseids, so you may see meteors from both radiants in the same session. The waxing crescent Moon sets early, leaving dark skies.

{# ── AUGUST ── #}
Eclipse
Partial Solar Eclipse
12 Aug

The headline astronomical event of 2026 for the UK. A total solar eclipse sweeps across Greenland, Iceland and northern Spain. From the UK, up to 89% of the Sun will be covered — with the deepest eclipse visible from western Cornwall.

Best viewing: The partial eclipse begins around 17:45 BST, reaching maximum coverage around 18:45 BST, and ending around 19:30 BST. It occurs during the early evening, with the Sun still reasonably high in the western sky.

UK visibility: Outstanding. Coverage ranges from approximately 89% in south-west Cornwall to around 80% in northern Scotland. The further south and west you are, the deeper the eclipse.

Tips: NEVER look directly at the Sun without certified solar eclipse glasses (ISO 12312-2). Regular sunglasses are not safe. A pinhole projector is a safe and fun alternative. This is a once-in-a-generation event for Cornwall — plan your viewing location well in advance.

Meteor Shower
Perseids Meteor Shower Peak
12–13 Aug

The Perseids are the most popular annual meteor shower, producing up to 100 meteors per hour under ideal conditions. They originate from Comet 109P/Swift–Tuttle and are renowned for bright fireballs. In 2026, the Perseid peak falls on the same day as the partial solar eclipse — a remarkable double event.

Best viewing: After midnight on 12 August into pre-dawn on 13 August. The radiant is in Perseus, rising high in the north-east. Watch the eclipse in the evening, then stay out for meteors!

UK visibility: Excellent from all UK locations. August’s warm evenings and the high radiant altitude make this the most comfortable meteor shower to observe.

Tips: In 2026, the waning crescent Moon will not be a problem — dark skies are expected for the peak. This could be an exceptional year for the Perseids. The shower is active 17 Jul – 24 Aug.

Full Moon
Full Sturgeon Moon
28 Aug

August’s full Moon is the Sturgeon Moon, named by Native American fishing tribes for the large sturgeon fish caught at this time of year in the Great Lakes. In 2026, this Moon coincides with a partial lunar eclipse visible from the UK.

Best viewing: Visible from late evening, rising in the south-east. Watch for the partial eclipse as part of the Moon enters Earth’s shadow.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. As summer progresses, full Moons begin to climb slightly higher again.

Tips: The partial lunar eclipse on the same night makes this a doubly special full Moon. See the eclipse card for detailed timings and viewing advice.

Eclipse
Partial Lunar Eclipse
28 Aug

A partial lunar eclipse is visible from the UK on the same night as the Sturgeon Moon. Part of the Moon passes through Earth’s dark umbral shadow, creating a distinctive “bite” out of the lunar disc.

Best viewing: The eclipse is visible during the evening and night hours. The Moon rises already partially eclipsed, so a clear eastern horizon is important.

UK visibility: Visible from the entire UK. This is the best lunar eclipse of 2026 for British observers, as the March total lunar eclipse was not visible from our time zone.

Tips: A partial lunar eclipse is a naked-eye event — no special equipment needed. Binoculars enhance the view, showing the curved edge of Earth’s shadow creeping across the lunar surface. A great event for beginners and families.

{# ── SEPTEMBER ── #}
Season
September Equinox
23 Sep

The autumnal equinox marks the start of astronomical autumn. Day and night are approximately equal, and from this date the nights grow longer than the days.

Best viewing: Sunrise and sunset occur due east and west respectively.

UK visibility: The equinox signals the return of longer nights — excellent news for astronomers and aurora hunters. Dark skies return to northern Scotland.

Tips: The equinox period is also prime time for aurora activity. Geomagnetic storms are statistically more common around the equinoxes due to the orientation of Earth’s magnetic field relative to the solar wind.

Planet
Neptune at Opposition
26 Sep

Neptune reaches opposition, shining at its brightest for the year at magnitude +7.8. It remains invisible to the naked eye but is reachable through binoculars and telescopes.

Best viewing: Visible most of the night in the constellation Pisces. Highest around midnight.

UK visibility: Good from all locations with optical aid. You’ll need at least 7×50 binoculars and a detailed star chart to identify it among the background stars.

Tips: Through a telescope at 100×+ magnification, Neptune appears as a tiny blue-green disc — distinct from the pinpoint stars around it. Its largest moon Triton can be spotted in larger amateur telescopes.

Full Moon
Full Harvest Moon
26 Sep

The Harvest Moon is the full Moon closest to the autumnal equinox. It is perhaps the most famous of all named full Moons, historically providing extra light for farmers gathering crops late into the evening.

Best viewing: Visible all night, rising around sunset. The Harvest Moon rises at nearly the same time on several consecutive evenings — the “Harvest Moon effect” — providing moonlit evenings for several nights in a row.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. The Moon climbs to a moderate altitude during the lengthening autumn nights.

Tips: The Harvest Moon often appears particularly large and orange at the horizon due to the “Moon illusion” combined with atmospheric scattering. A wonderful opportunity for landscape photography at moonrise.

{# ── OCTOBER ── #}
Planet
Saturn at Opposition
4 Oct

Saturn reaches opposition — directly opposite the Sun — making it visible all night, rising at sunset and setting at sunrise. It shines at its brightest for the year at magnitude +0.5.

Best viewing: Visible all night, highest in the south around midnight. Saturn will be in the constellation Pisces.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. Opposition occurs when Earth is closest to Saturn, making its rings and moons most visible through a telescope.

Tips: 2026 is a remarkable year for Saturn — the rings are nearly edge-on as Saturn approaches its ring-plane crossing. Through a telescope, the rings appear as a thin line rather than the familiar wide ovals. This geometric alignment happens only once every ~15 years. Even a small telescope (60mm+) will show this unusual view.

Meteor Shower
Draconids Meteor Shower
8–9 Oct

The Draconids are an unusual meteor shower — they are best observed in the early evening rather than after midnight, as the radiant in the constellation Draco is highest in the sky before midnight from UK latitudes.

Best viewing: Early evening on 8 October, looking north-west. Rates are typically modest at around 10 meteors per hour, but the Draconids have produced spectacular storms in the past (most notably in 1933 and 2011).

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations. The radiant is circumpolar and high in the evening sky, giving northern latitudes an advantage over the tropics.

Tips: The parent comet is 21P/Giacobini–Zinner. Draconid meteors are among the slowest at just 20 km/s, making them appear to drift gracefully across the sky rather than streak. The waning crescent Moon won’t interfere.

Meteor Shower
Orionids Meteor Shower Peak
21–22 Oct

The Orionids are the second meteor shower produced by Halley’s Comet debris (the Eta Aquariids being the first). They produce around 20 meteors per hour at peak.

Best viewing: After midnight on 21 October into pre-dawn on 22 October. The radiant is near Betelgeuse in Orion, which rises in the east.

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations. Orion is well-placed in the autumn pre-dawn sky.

Tips: Orionid meteors are fast (66 km/s) and often produce persistent trains that glow for several seconds. The waning crescent Moon will not be a problem for this shower.

Full Moon
Full Hunter’s Moon
26 Oct

The Hunter’s Moon is the first full Moon after the Harvest Moon. Historically, it marked the time to hunt and stockpile meat before winter.

Best viewing: Visible all night, rising around sunset. October full Moons often appear large and orange at the horizon due to the shallow angle of the ecliptic.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. The Moon climbs higher each month now as we approach winter.

Tips: The Hunter’s Moon rises at nearly the same time on successive evenings (like the Harvest Moon), so you get several evenings of “full-looking” moonrise in a row.

{# ── NOVEMBER ── #}
Meteor Shower
Leonids Meteor Shower Peak
17–18 Nov

The Leonids are famous for producing periodic meteor storms (most recently in 1999 and 2001). In a normal year, expect around 15 meteors per hour. The parent comet is 55P/Tempel–Tuttle.

Best viewing: After midnight on 17 November into pre-dawn on 18 November. The radiant is in Leo, rising in the east.

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations. Leo is well above the horizon by 1am.

Tips: Leonid meteors are among the fastest at 71 km/s, often producing bright fireballs with vivid colours. The waning crescent Moon rises late and won’t interfere significantly.

Full Moon
Full Beaver Moon (Supermoon)
24 Nov

November’s full Moon is the Beaver Moon, named for the time when beaver traps were traditionally set before waterways froze over. This is also a Supermoon — the Moon is near perigee, appearing slightly larger and brighter than average.

Best viewing: Visible all night, rising in the east around sunset. November’s Moon climbs high in the sky during the long autumn night.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. This is the second of three Supermoons in 2026 (January, November, December).

Tips: A Supermoon on a crisp November night is a beautiful sight. The high altitude and long winter nights give extended observing time. Compare its apparent size to the Micromoon back in June.

Planet
Uranus at Opposition
25 Nov

Uranus reaches opposition, shining at magnitude +5.6 — technically just within naked-eye range under perfect dark-sky conditions, but realistically requiring binoculars to identify.

Best viewing: Visible most of the night in the constellation Taurus. Highest around midnight. At opposition, Uranus is at its closest and brightest for the year.

UK visibility: Good from all locations with binoculars or a telescope. You’ll need a star chart or planetarium app to distinguish it from surrounding stars.

Tips: Through a telescope at 100×+ magnification, Uranus appears as a small pale blue-green disc. Unlike the pinpoint stars around it, Uranus shows a definite disc shape. Try to spot it naked-eye from a genuinely dark site — it’s a rewarding challenge.

{# ── DECEMBER ── #}
Meteor Shower
Geminids Meteor Shower Peak
13–14 Dec

The Geminids are widely considered the best meteor shower of the year, producing up to 150 multicoloured meteors per hour under ideal conditions. Unusually, they originate from an asteroid — 3200 Phaethon — rather than a comet.

Best viewing: From around 9pm on 13 December through pre-dawn on 14 December. The radiant in Gemini is well-placed by mid-evening, rising in the east-northeast.

UK visibility: Excellent from all UK locations. The Geminid radiant rises early, giving a long observing window. Rates increase steadily through the night.

Tips: In 2026, the Moon will be a waning crescent and will not interfere — this could be one of the best Geminid displays in years. Geminid meteors are slower than Perseids and come in a range of colours — white, yellow, green, and blue. Bundle up warmly for December’s cold, clear nights.

Season
December Solstice — Shortest Day
21 Dec

The winter solstice is the shortest day and longest night of the year — the Sun reaches its lowest noontime altitude. From London, there are only about 7 hours 50 minutes of daylight.

Best viewing: The longest night of the year — perfect for extended observing sessions. Astronomical darkness begins early and lasts until morning.

UK visibility: The solstice marks the peak of the observing season. Over 16 hours of darkness from southern England, and even more from Scotland.

Tips: Wrap up warm and make the most of the long night. The winter Milky Way is spectacular, Orion dominates the south, and the aurora season continues. Many observing groups hold all-night solstice events.

Meteor Shower
Ursids Meteor Shower Peak
22–23 Dec

The Ursids are the final meteor shower of the year, producing around 5–10 meteors per hour. They radiate from near the star Kochab in Ursa Minor (the Little Bear).

Best viewing: Pre-dawn hours on 22–23 December. The radiant is circumpolar from the UK, meaning it’s visible all night.

UK visibility: Good from all UK locations — the circumpolar radiant gives UK observers an advantage over southern latitudes.

Tips: Though modest in rates, the Ursids occasionally produce unexpected outbursts. The waxing crescent Moon sets early, leaving excellent dark skies for the longest nights of the year. A fitting end to the 2026 observing calendar.

Full Moon
Full Cold Moon (Supermoon)
24 Dec

The final full Moon of 2026 is the Cold Moon — and it’s the closest Supermoon of the year, appearing at its largest and brightest. Falling on Christmas Eve, it promises a spectacular moonlit holiday night.

Best viewing: Visible all night, reaching a very high altitude as it crosses the sky. From northern UK locations, it passes almost directly overhead.

UK visibility: Excellent from all locations. As the closest Supermoon of 2026 (approximately 221,667 miles from Earth), it will appear noticeably larger and brighter than the June Micromoon.

Tips: A Christmas Eve Supermoon is a magical way to end the year. Step outside after dinner and look up — the Moon will be brilliant, casting sharp shadows across frosty landscapes. The long winter night ahead is ideal for both Moon-gazing and spotting the winter constellations.
